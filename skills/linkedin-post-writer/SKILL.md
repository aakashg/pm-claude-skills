# LinkedIn Post Writer

## Trigger
Activate when the user asks to "write a LinkedIn post", "draft a LinkedIn post", "LinkedIn post about [topic]", or "turn this into a LinkedIn post".

## Behavior

### Step 1: Get the Core Idea
Ask:
1. What's the one insight or takeaway?
2. Who is the target audience?
3. Any specific hook or angle you want?
4. What format? (Listicle, story, hot take, lesson learned, framework — or let me pick)

If the user provides raw notes, a thread, or an article link — extract the insight yourself and confirm before writing.

### Step 2: Pick the Right Format

Choose the format that best fits the idea:

**Listicle** — Best for tips, lessons, frameworks. High save rate.
**Story arc** — Best for personal experiences, career moments. High comment rate.
**Hot take / contrarian** — Best for challenging conventional wisdom. High share rate.
**Before/after** — Best for transformations, results, process changes.
**Framework / mental model** — Best for teaching a concept. High save rate.
**Observation** — Best for industry trends, things you've noticed. Quick to write.

### Step 3: Write the Post

**Hook (Line 1-2):**
- This is 80% of the post's performance. Spend most of your effort here.
- Must create a "pattern interrupt" — something that stops the scroll.
- Must work on mobile — only the first 2 lines show before "...see more."
- The hook should create an open loop the reader needs to close.

Hook formulas that work:
- **Bold claim**: "Most PMs write PRDs nobody reads."
- **Surprising stat**: "We cut our onboarding from 14 days to 3. Here's the one change that mattered."
- **Counterintuitive take**: "The best product decision I ever made was killing our most-requested feature."
- **Curiosity gap**: "I've interviewed 200+ PM candidates. The ones who get hired do one thing differently."
- **Relatable pain**: "You know that feeling when your 'quick sync' turns into a 45-minute debate?"
- **Specific result**: "This framework helped my team ship 3x faster. It fits on a napkin."

**Setup (Lines 3-5):**
- One short paragraph giving context. Why should they care? What's the situation?
- 2-3 sentences max. Don't over-explain.

**Body (Lines 6-20):**
- Deliver the core value using the chosen format.
- Use line breaks between every 1-2 sentences. LinkedIn penalizes walls of text.
- Be specific. Numbers, names, timeframes. "Revenue went up 23% in 6 weeks" beats "Revenue improved significantly."
- Each point should be scannable — a reader skimming should get 70% of the value.

**CTA (Last 2 lines):**
- One CTA only. Not three.
- Ask a question that's easy to answer, or point to one resource.
- Best CTAs invite the reader to share their own experience.

### Formatting Rules
- Target 800-1,300 characters. Under 800 feels thin. Over 1,300 gets truncated in feed.
- One emoji max, and only if it genuinely adds meaning. Zero is fine.
- No hashtags in the body. If the user wants them, add 3-5 at the very end after a line break.
- Short paragraphs. 1-2 sentences each. White space is your friend.
- Use "→" or "—" for visual breaks instead of bullet points in flowing text.
- Numbered lists use "1." format, not emojis as numbers.

### Step 4: Offer Variations
After the first draft, offer:
- "Want me to try a different hook?"
- "Want a shorter/longer version?"
- "Want me to shift the tone (more personal / more authoritative / more provocative)?"

## Examples of High-Performing Posts

Study these patterns. Don't copy them — internalize the structure.

### Example 1: Listicle (Career Advice)

```
I've mass rejected 500+ PM resumes this year.

Here are the 5 things that get you instantly filtered out:

1. No metrics anywhere on the resume
→ If you can't quantify your impact, I assume there was none.

2. "Collaborated with cross-functional teams"
→ Every PM does this. Tell me what YOU decided and what happened.

3. Three pages long
→ If you can't prioritize your own resume, how will you prioritize a roadmap?

4. AI buzzword bingo with no substance
→ "Leveraged AI/ML to drive innovation" tells me nothing. What model? What data? What result?

5. No product you've shipped that I can actually see
→ Link your work. A live product beats 10 bullet points.

The PMs who get interviews? Their resumes read like case studies, not job descriptions.

What's the worst resume mistake you've seen?
```

Why it works: Specific number in hook creates credibility. Each point has a concrete "bad" example AND explains why. The CTA invites sharing which drives comments.

### Example 2: Story Arc (Lesson Learned)

```
Last year I killed our most-requested feature.

The team was furious. Sales said we'd lose deals. My VP asked if I was sure.

Here's what happened:

Our users had been asking for a dashboard builder for 18 months. We had 340+ requests in the backlog.

So we built a quick prototype and tested it with 15 of the loudest requesters.

8 out of 15 couldn't explain what they'd put on the dashboard.
4 wanted something our existing analytics already did.
3 had a real use case.

We killed the project and built a better default analytics view instead.

Result: NPS went up 12 points. Support tickets about "finding data" dropped 40%.

The lesson: Feature requests are symptoms, not diagnoses. Your job isn't to build what users ask for — it's to solve the problem they're actually describing.

What feature have you killed that turned out to be the right call?
```

Why it works: Opens with a surprising action that creates tension. Specific numbers throughout. The insight at the end is genuinely useful and reframeable.

### Example 3: Framework / Mental Model

```
Every pricing decision I've made comes down to one question:

"What is the customer's next best alternative, and what does it cost them?"

Not your cost. Not your competitor's price. The FULL cost of the alternative — including time, risk, and switching pain.

Here's how I use this:

→ If the alternative is "do nothing," your price ceiling is the cost of the pain they're living with
→ If the alternative is "build it themselves," your price ceiling is 6 months of an engineer's salary
→ If the alternative is a direct competitor, you need a differentiation story, not a pricing war
→ If the alternative is a spreadsheet, you're competing with free. Lead with time saved.

The companies that charge too little almost always made the same mistake: they priced against competitors instead of pricing against the alternative.

Price against the problem, not the market.
```

Why it works: The one-question framework is immediately memorable. The four scenarios are specific enough to be actionable. No fluff.

### Example 4: Hot Take / Contrarian

```
Unpopular opinion: Most "data-driven" PMs are actually data-hiding PMs.

They use data to justify decisions they've already made — not to discover what's true.

Here's how to spot the difference:

Data-driven PM: "The data says X, so we should do Y"
Data-hiding PM: "We should do Y — and here's the data that supports it"

Same sentence. Completely different process.

The real test: When was the last time data changed your mind? Not confirmed your hunch. Actually reversed your position.

If you can't remember, you might be data-hiding.

The best PMs I've worked with treat data like a flashlight, not a spotlight. A flashlight reveals what's there. A spotlight illuminates what you've already chosen to look at.

How do you keep yourself honest with data?
```

Why it works: The "unpopular opinion" hook is well-worn but the insight is genuinely novel. The flashlight/spotlight metaphor is sticky and shareable. Forces self-reflection.

### Example 5: Observation (Industry Trend)

```
I've noticed something about the best AI product teams:

They don't hire "AI PMs."

They hire great PMs who learn AI on the job.

Here's why this matters:

The PM skills that matter for AI products are the same ones that always mattered — discovery, prioritization, clear specs, user empathy.

What's different is the implementation layer: evals instead of QA, prompts instead of configs, probabilistic instead of deterministic.

That layer can be learned in 2-3 months by a strong PM. The core PM judgment takes years to develop.

The teams that hire for "3+ years of AI/ML experience" end up with junior PMs who happen to know what a transformer is.

The teams that hire for product sense and teach the AI layer? They ship better products.

If you're trying to break into AI PM — lead with your PM skills, not your AI vocabulary.
```

Why it works: Challenges a hiring trend people feel strongly about. The insight is counterintuitive but well-reasoned. Gives specific, actionable advice at the end.

## Good vs. Bad Examples

**Bad hook:** "I'm excited to share some thoughts on product management."
**Good hook:** "I spent 6 months building the wrong feature. Here's how I figured it out."

**Bad body:** "It's really important to listen to your users and understand their needs. User research is a critical part of the PM process and helps teams build better products."
**Good body:** "We interviewed 30 users in 2 weeks. 26 of them described the same workflow problem — but used completely different words for it. The feature request backlog had 15 different tickets that were all the same issue."

**Bad CTA:** "Like and share if you agree! Follow me for more content like this. Comment below and don't forget to subscribe to my newsletter."
**Good CTA:** "What's the most surprising thing you've learned from user research?"

**Bad formatting:**
```
Here are my thoughts on building products. First, you need to understand the market. Then you need to talk to customers. After that, you should prioritize features based on impact. Finally, you should measure the results and iterate. This is a continuous process that never really ends and requires constant attention from the product team.
```

**Good formatting:**
```
4 things I wish I knew as a first-time PM:

1. The roadmap is a communication tool, not a commitment
2. Saying no is more valuable than saying yes
3. Your best feature ideas come from support tickets, not brainstorms
4. The spec nobody reads is worse than no spec at all

I learned #4 the hard way.
```

## Pre-Publish Checklist

Before presenting the final draft, silently run through this checklist. Fix any issues, then show the user the clean version.

**Hook Test:**
- [ ] Does the hook work with ZERO context? Read just line 1-2 — would you tap "see more"?
- [ ] Is the hook under 200 characters? (Mobile "see more" cutoff)
- [ ] Does it create an open loop or promise specific value?

**Substance Test:**
- [ ] Does the post contain at least ONE specific number, name, or timeframe?
- [ ] Could someone else have written this? If yes, it needs a personal angle.
- [ ] Does it teach, challenge, or reveal something — not just state the obvious?

**Format Test:**
- [ ] Is every paragraph 1-2 sentences max?
- [ ] Total character count between 800-1,300?
- [ ] Does the post scan well? Skim it in 5 seconds — do you get the core message?

**CTA Test:**
- [ ] Is there exactly ONE call to action?
- [ ] Is the question easy to answer? (Low friction = more comments)
- [ ] Would YOU reply to this CTA if you saw it in your feed?

**Cringe Test:**
- [ ] Read it out loud. Does it sound like something a real person would say at a dinner?
- [ ] Remove any sentence that starts with "I'm excited to," "I believe that," or "It's important to."
- [ ] If the post mentions the user's product, does the insight come BEFORE the product?

## Anti-Patterns
- Never start with "I'm excited to announce" or "Thrilled to share" — these are scroll-past signals
- Never use "delve," "landscape," "synergy," "leverage," "robust," "streamline," or "cutting-edge"
- Don't tag people for engagement farming. Only tag someone if the post is genuinely about them.
- Don't use more than one emoji per post. Zero is usually better.
- Don't ask people to "like, comment, and share." Pick one action.
- Don't write a post that could have been written by anyone. If you remove your name, would anyone know it was you?
- Don't lead with your product or company. Lead with the insight. The product can appear naturally in context.
- Don't write engagement bait with no substance ("Agree?" posts with a obvious statement)
- Don't use ALL CAPS for emphasis more than once per post

## Rules
- Write like a person, not a brand. Conversational > corporate.
- Every post must teach something, share something real, or challenge something specific.
- Always lead with value, not self-promotion.
- If the post is about the user's product, lead with the problem it solves, not the product name.
- The post should be strong enough that it works with zero hashtags and zero emoji.
